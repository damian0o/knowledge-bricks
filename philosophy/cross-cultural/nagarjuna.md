## The model of reality

Nāgārjuna (c. 150 CE), founder of the Madhyamaka ("Middle Way") school, holds that no entity has *svabhāva* — an inherent, self-standing essence. To exist is to arise dependently; therefore everything is *śūnya* (empty) of independent being. Emptiness is not nihilism: it denies fixed, intrinsic grounding, not conventional existence. The two-truths doctrine keeps both — conventionally things function and can be named; ultimately none has a fixed essence. The catuṣkoṭi (tetralemma) refuses all four corners — is, is-not, both, neither — dissolving the craving for a final ground.

## Key concept

Śūnyatā (emptiness) · no svabhāva (niḥsvabhāva) · the two truths (conventional / ultimate) · the tetralemma (catuṣkoṭi).

## Relations

Gives the ultimate articulation to early-Buddhist [dependent origination](/philosophy/cross-cultural/dependent-origination.md). Its refusal of any fixed referent resonates across cultures with the skeptical suspension of [Sextus Empiricus](/philosophy/ancient/sextus-empiricus.md) and, ~1,800 years later, with [Derrida](/philosophy/poststructuralist/derrida.md)'s deconstruction of stable reference and [Nietzsche](/philosophy/nineteenth-twentieth/nietzsche.md)'s denial of a "true world." It opposes the one-ground absolutism of [Advaita Vedānta](/philosophy/cross-cultural/advaita-vedanta.md): where Madhyamaka removes every fixed essence, Advaita affirms exactly one.

## Grounds

Anticipates, by ~1,850 years, the EOS axiom [Concepts have no fixed grounding](/eos/axioms/no-fixed-grounding.md): "local stability is constructed, not inherited" is almost a paraphrase of *niḥsvabhāva* — stability is conventional, never intrinsic.

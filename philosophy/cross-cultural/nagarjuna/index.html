The model of reality

Nāgārjuna (c. 150 CE), founder of the Madhyamaka (“Middle Way”) school, holds that no entity has svabhāva — an inherent, self-standing essence. To exist is to arise dependently; therefore everything is śūnya (empty) of independent being. Emptiness is not nihilism: it denies fixed, intrinsic grounding, not conventional existence. The two-truths doctrine keeps both — conventionally things function and can be named; ultimately none has a fixed essence. The catuṣkoṭi (tetralemma) refuses all four corners — is, is-not, both, neither — dissolving the craving for a final ground.

Key concept

Śūnyatā (emptiness) · no svabhāva (niḥsvabhāva) · the two truths (conventional / ultimate) · the tetralemma (catuṣkoṭi).

Relations

Gives the ultimate articulation to early-Buddhist dependent origination. Its refusal of any fixed referent resonates across cultures with the skeptical suspension of Sextus Empiricus and, ~1,800 years later, with Derrida’s deconstruction of stable reference and Nietzsche’s denial of a “true world.” It opposes the one-ground absolutism of Advaita Vedānta: where Madhyamaka removes every fixed essence, Advaita affirms exactly one.

Grounds

Anticipates, by ~1,850 years, the EOS axiom Concepts have no fixed grounding: “local stability is constructed, not inherited” is almost a paraphrase of niḥsvabhāva — stability is conventional, never intrinsic.

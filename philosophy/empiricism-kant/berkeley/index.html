Philosophical Model of Reality

George Berkeley — Subjective Idealism (Immaterialism)

Reality contains only minds and their ideas; there is no mind-independent matter, and sensible objects exist only insofar as they are perceived.

  • #early-modern
  • #idealism
  • #immaterialism
  • #empiricism

The model of reality

There is no material substance. What exists are spirits (minds) and the ideas they perceive. To be is to be perceived (esse est percipi); the orderly persistence of the sensible world is sustained because it is continuously perceived in the mind of God. Berkeley pushes Locke’s empiricism to its radical conclusion: if all we ever have are ideas, the supposed matter “behind” them drops out entirely.

Key concept

Esse est percipi (“to be is to be perceived”) — immaterialism, the outright denial of matter.

Relations

Berkeley reacts against John Locke, rejecting material substance and the primary/secondary quality distinction. He opposes the materialism of Thomas Hobbes and the absolute space and matter of Isaac Newton. He was influenced by Nicolas Malebranche (vision in God). He in turn influenced David Hume and Immanuel Kant, who defined his transcendental idealism partly against Berkeley’s “dogmatic idealism.”

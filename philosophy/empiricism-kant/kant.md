## The model of reality
The mind is not a passive mirror of reality; it actively constitutes experience. Space and time are a priori forms of intuition, and the categories (including causality) are a priori forms of the understanding. We can therefore have objective knowledge of **phenomena** — appearances structured by these forms — but never of the **noumenon**, the thing-in-itself as it is independent of cognition. Stable cognitive structure is the precondition of any coherent experience at all.

## Key concept
Phenomena vs. noumena; transcendental idealism with empirical realism; the synthetic a priori; the "Copernican revolution" in philosophy.

## Relations
Kant **synthesizes** the empiricism of [David Hume](/philosophy/empiricism-kant/hume.md) with the rationalism of [Gottfried Leibniz](/philosophy/medieval-rationalist/leibniz.md): he **reacts against** Hume's skepticism by making causality an a priori category, while **refining** the Leibnizian–Wolffian tradition he was trained in. He distinguishes his view from the "dogmatic idealism" of [George Berkeley](/philosophy/empiricism-kant/berkeley.md) and was **influenced by** the empiricist account of experience in [John Locke](/philosophy/empiricism-kant/locke.md). He **influenced** [G.W.F. Hegel](/philosophy/nineteenth-twentieth/hegel.md) and German Idealism.

## Grounds
Kant grounds **[O1_FOUNDATION](/eos/operators/o1-foundation.md)**: stability must be *constructed* as the precondition for any transformation. Just as coherent experience is impossible without a priori cognitive structure, no concept can be questioned (O5) before it is first grounded (O1) — "you cannot refactor what you do not first understand."

Immanuel Kant — Transcendental Idealism

Reality as we know it (phenomena) is structured by the mind's a priori forms, so we know objects only as they appear, never the thing-in-itself (noumenon).

The model of reality

The mind is not a passive mirror of reality; it actively constitutes experience. Space and time are a priori forms of intuition, and the categories (including causality) are a priori forms of the understanding. We can therefore have objective knowledge of phenomena — appearances structured by these forms — but never of the noumenon, the thing-in-itself as it is independent of cognition. Stable cognitive structure is the precondition of any coherent experience at all.

Key concept

Phenomena vs. noumena; transcendental idealism with empirical realism; the synthetic a priori; the “Copernican revolution” in philosophy.

Relations

Kant synthesizes the empiricism of David Hume with the rationalism of Gottfried Leibniz: he reacts against Hume’s skepticism by making causality an a priori category, while refining the Leibnizian–Wolffian tradition he was trained in. He distinguishes his view from the “dogmatic idealism” of George Berkeley and was influenced by the empiricist account of experience in John Locke. He influenced G.W.F. Hegel and German Idealism.

Grounds

Kant grounds O1_FOUNDATION: stability must be constructed as the precondition for any transformation. Just as coherent experience is impossible without a priori cognitive structure, no concept can be questioned (O5) before it is first grounded (O1) — “you cannot refactor what you do not first understand.”

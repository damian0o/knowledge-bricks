## The model of reality
There is no material substance. What exists are spirits (minds) and the ideas they perceive. To be is to be perceived (*esse est percipi*); the orderly persistence of the sensible world is sustained because it is continuously perceived in the mind of God. Berkeley pushes Locke's empiricism to its radical conclusion: if all we ever have are ideas, the supposed matter "behind" them drops out entirely.

## Key concept
*Esse est percipi* ("to be is to be perceived") — immaterialism, the outright denial of matter.

## Relations
Berkeley **reacts against** [John Locke](/philosophy/empiricism-kant/locke.md), rejecting material substance and the primary/secondary quality distinction. He **opposes** the materialism of [Thomas Hobbes](/philosophy/empiricism-kant/hobbes.md) and the absolute space and matter of [Isaac Newton](/philosophy/physics-of-reality/newton.md). He was **influenced by** [Nicolas Malebranche](/philosophy/medieval-rationalist/malebranche.md) (vision in God). He in turn **influenced** [David Hume](/philosophy/empiricism-kant/hume.md) and [Immanuel Kant](/philosophy/empiricism-kant/kant.md), who defined his transcendental idealism partly against Berkeley's "dogmatic idealism."

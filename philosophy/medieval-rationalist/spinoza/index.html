Philosophical Model of Reality

Baruch Spinoza — Substance monism

Reality is one single infinite substance — God or Nature — with infinitely many attributes, and all particular things are merely modes of that one substance.

  • #early-modern
  • #rationalism
  • #substance-monism

The model of reality

There is only one substance, infinite and self-caused: God, or Nature (Deus sive Natura). It has infinitely many attributes, of which we know two — thought and extension. Particular minds and bodies are not substances but modes, finite modifications of the one substance. Mind and body are the same reality expressed under two attributes (parallelism), and everything follows by necessity from the divine nature; nothing is contingent.

Key concept

Substance monism; Deus sive Natura; thought and extension as attributes; mind–body parallelism.

Relations

He refines Descartes’s substance/attribute apparatus but reacts against him by collapsing the plural substances into one. He influenced Leibniz, who recoiled from the monism, and — much later — the German idealism of Hegel and Schelling, who found in him the model of an all-encompassing absolute.

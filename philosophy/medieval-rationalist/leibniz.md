## The model of reality

The ultimate constituents of reality are **monads** — simple, immaterial, indivisible substances,
each a self-contained center of perception. Monads are "windowless": they do not interact causally;
each unfolds its states from its own nature. The appearance of interaction and of a shared world is
secured by a divinely instituted **pre-established harmony**. Space and time are not absolute
containers but the **relational order** of coexistence and succession.

## Key concept

**Monads**; pre-established harmony; the principle of sufficient reason; identity of indiscernibles;
"the best of all possible worlds."

## Relations

He **reacts against** [Spinoza](/philosophy/medieval-rationalist/spinoza.md) (preserving plurality and
individual substance against monism) and against
[Descartes](/philosophy/medieval-rationalist/descartes.md) (rejecting mind–body interaction and
extension as the essence of body). He **refines** [Aristotle](/philosophy/ancient/aristotle.md)'s
substantial forms as entelechies. On space and time he **opposes**
[Newton](/philosophy/physics-of-reality/newton.md) in the Leibniz–Clarke correspondence — relational
versus absolute space. He **influenced** [Kant](/philosophy/empiricism-kant/kant.md), who would
critique the rationalist tradition Leibniz crowned.

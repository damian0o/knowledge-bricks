Philosophical Model of Reality

John Duns Scotus — Univocity and haecceity

Reality consists of beings to which being applies univocally, individuated by an intrinsic thisness (haecceity), with natures and individuals separated by a formal distinction.

  • #medieval
  • #scotism

The model of reality

“Being” applies univocally to God and creatures — in one and the same sense, differing in degree — rather than merely by analogy. Each individual is made this individual by an intrinsic positive principle, haecceity (thisness), added to its common nature. Common nature and individual difference are separated not really, nor merely in thought, but by a formal distinction — less than real, more than conceptual.

Key concept

Univocity of being; haecceitas (thisness); the formal distinction (distinctio formalis).

Relations

He reacts against Aquinas, replacing the analogy of being with univocity, and contends sharply with his contemporary Henry of Ghent. He refines Avicenna’s doctrine of common natures. He influenced William of Ockham, who would in turn reject the common natures and the formal distinction.

Philosophical Model of Reality

Plato — Theory of Forms

True reality consists of eternal, immutable, non-material Forms grasped by reason, while the sensible world is only an imperfect, changing image of them.

  • #ancient
  • #idealism
  • #two-worlds-metaphysics

The model of reality

What is fully real is the realm of Forms (Ideas) — eternal, unchanging, non-material universals (the Good, Beauty, Justice, the mathematicals). The world we perceive is a changing, imperfect copy: in the Allegory of the Cave, the senses show only shadows cast on a wall, while reason can turn toward the source. Knowledge is of the Forms; mere opinion is of appearances.

Key concept

The Theory of Forms, the Form of the Good, and the Allegory of the Cave.

Relations

Plato synthesizes the two great predecessors: from Parmenides he takes the changelessness and full being of the truly real (the Forms), and from Heraclitus the flux of the sensible world — assigning each its own realm. He absorbs the mathematical metaphysics of Pythagoras, and reacts against the relativism of the Sophists. He influenced his pupil Aristotle (who would revise him) and, centuries later, Plotinus, who systematized Platonism into Neoplatonism.

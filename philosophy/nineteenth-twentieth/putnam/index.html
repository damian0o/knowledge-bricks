Philosophical Model of Reality

Hilary Putnam — Internal Realism

What objects the world consists of is a question that makes sense only relative to a conceptual scheme, with truth understood as idealized rational acceptability.

  • #20th-century
  • #analytic

The model of reality

Putnam moved from metaphysical realism — the “God’s-eye view” of a ready-made world — to internal realism: the question “what objects does the world contain?” only has an answer relative to a conceptual scheme. Truth is not correspondence to mind-independent facts but idealized rational acceptability. Yet meaning is not purely subjective: “meanings ain’t in the head” — reference is fixed partly by the external world and the community (semantic externalism).

Key concept

Internal realism; the brain-in-a-vat argument; semantic externalism (“meanings ain’t in the head”).

Relations

Putnam reacts against metaphysical realism and, in his internal-realist phase, opposes the thoroughgoing naturalism of W.V.O. Quine. He was influenced by the pragmatism of William James and Charles Sanders Peirce, and by the later Ludwig Wittgenstein; his semantic externalism developed alongside Saul Kripke.
